# Google and Meta Behavioral Masterclass

While Amazon uses strict Leadership Principles, Google and Meta have their own distinct cultures. Understanding what behavior these companies penalize versus what they reward is how you pass their "Jedi" behavioral rounds.

---

## 🔵 Google: "Googlyness" and Ambiguity
Google's behavioral loop focuses heavily on **Leadership** (even for individual contributors), **Handling Ambiguity**, and a cultural fit known as **Googlyness**.

### 1. Handling Ambiguity 
*Google is chaotic. They want engineers who don't freeze when requirements are completely missing.*
- **What they want:** You took an extremely vague problem ("Make the search bar faster"), researched it yourself, proposed 3 solutions with trade-offs, and drove the implementation without waiting for someone to tell you what to do.
- **Common Questions:**
  - *Tell me about a time you had to work on a project with vague or constantly changing requirements.*
  - *Give me an example of a time you had to make a decision without having all the facts.*

### 2. Leadership (Without Authority)
*You don't need the title of "Manager" to lead a project.*
- **What they want:** You stepped up to organize a chaotic situation. You mentored a junior developer. You influenced a senior engineer to change their mind on an architecture decision.
- **Common Questions:**
  - *Tell me about a time you led a project where you weren't the manager.*
  - *Tell me about a time you had to convince a group to adopt your idea.*

### 3. "Googlyness"
*Doing the right thing, valuing diversity, and being intellectually humble.*
- **What they want:** You helped clean up tech debt that nobody asked you to clean up. You admitted when you were wrong in a technical design meeting. You actively contributed to a healthy team culture.
- **Common Questions:**
  - *Tell me about a time you helped a teammate who was struggling.*
  - *Tell me about a time you pushed for an inclusive environment or product feature.*

## ♾️ Meta (Facebook): The Core Values
Meta is famous for a "Move Fast" hacker culture. They value speed, impact, and direct feedback.

### 1. Move Fast & Be Bold
*Meta prefers shipping a v1.0 and iterating based on data over spending 6 months perfecting a feature.*
- **What they want:** You took a calculated risk to launch something quickly. You circumvented a slow, bureaucratic process to get an urgent feature out. You prefer action over "analysis paralysis."
- **Common Questions:**
  - *Tell me about a time you had to make a fast decision with incomplete data.*
  - *Tell me about a time you took a massive risk on a project. (It's OK if it failed, as long as you learned from it).*

### 2. Focus on Impact
*Don't build cool tech just because it's cool. Build things that move metrics.*
- **What they want:** You deprioritized a highly technical refactor because it wouldn't actually improve the user experience or business metrics. You always measure success with real numbers (latency reduction, $ saved, click-through-rate).
- **Common Questions:**
  - *Tell me about a time you decided NOT to build a feature.*
  - *How do you prioritize your engineering tasks during a sprint?*

### 3. Openness and Direct Feedback
*Meta is extremely direct. They don't have time for office politics or indirect communication.*
- **What they want:** You gave tough, critical feedback to a colleague, but did it constructively. You received harsh feedback on your PR, didn't get offended, and improved the code.
- **Common Questions:**
  - *Tell me about a time you gave difficult feedback to a peer or manager.*
  - *Tell me about a time you received constructive criticism. How did you react?*
